It is a well-settled and consistently affirmed principle of service jurisprudence that the Statement of Imputation (ANNEXURE-II) is purely ancillary and explanatory in nature. Its legal function is confined to elucidating the factual background, particulars, and circumstances in support of the misconduct already and expressly alleged in the corresponding Article of Charge (ANNEXURE-I). The Statement of Imputation does not possess an independent or substantive character, nor can it operate as a source of charge in itself.

In law, the Statement of Imputation cannot travel beyond the Article of Charge (Annexure-I). It cannot be utilised as a vehicle to:

introduce new or additional allegations,

expand or modify the scope of the charge,

impute distinct acts, omissions, or elements of misconduct, or

supply missing ingredients which do not form part of the charge as formally framed.

Judicial pronouncements of the Supreme Court, High Courts, and the Central Administrative Tribunal have consistently held that any attempt to enlarge, supplement, or alter the charge through the Statement of Imputation is impermissible in law. Such an approach causes serious prejudice to the Charged Officer, undermines the requirement of certainty in disciplinary proceedings, and vitiates the inquiry at its very inception.

The Charged Officer is entitled, as a matter of legal right, to know with precision, certainty, and clarity the exact charge he is required to meet. This certainty must emanate strictly and exclusively from the Article of Charge itself, and cannot be derived, inferred, or supplemented from explanatory material appended thereafter.

In the aforesaid legal backdrop, it becomes necessary to undertake a careful, structured, and comparative examination of each Article of Charge (ANNEXURE-I) vis-à-vis its corresponding Statement of Imputation (ANNEXURE-II) in the present case. Such scrutiny must objectively determine whether the imputations:

remain confined to explaining and particularising the charge as framed; or

impermissibly travel beyond the charge by introducing new facts, allegations, acts, omissions, or dimensions of misconduct not contained in the Article of Charge.

Accordingly, each Article of Charge is required to be examined independently, and wherever the Statement of Imputation, in substance or effect, imputes a fresh, additional, or expanded charge, such deviation must be clearly identified, articulated, and recorded in a point-wise manner, as the same strikes at the very root of the disciplinary proceedings.
